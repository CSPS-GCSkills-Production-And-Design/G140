Performance Management – Application Scenarios

Test your knowledge

A few things you should know before you begin:

You may complete the scenario more than once in order to gauge the consequences of making different decisions.

During the scenario, you will be presented with different situations and asked to make the best performance management decision.

You will be awarded points for each choice you make: 1 point for a poor decision, 5 points for an acceptable decision, and 10 points for the best decision.

You can only move forward.

You take out Julie’s performance agreement and start rating her performance against the criteria within the document.

You call Julie in for a meeting and hand over her performance agreement. She is surprised and put off.

You share the revised performance agreement with Julie.

Julie is surprised and immediately put off.

You think to yourself: I think it is time for a new strategy.

Julie asks: Why did you immediately jump to revising my performance agreement and not discuss the issue with me?

You ask Julie: Can we schedule a meeting for tomorrow? I would like us to speak as soon as possible.

Julie thinks to herself: I cannot believe this is happening!

After checking on the TBS Web site about how to proceed when dealing with discipline, you call up your labour relations representative.

She asks you to explain the facts and provide documentation on the situation.

You tell your labour relations representative: How many times did you provide feedback and meet with Julie in a formal performance meeting capacity?

Your labour relations representative answers: I had only one feedback session with her, and we have never had a formal performance meeting.

Your labour relations representative continues: Are you aware of what constitutes culpable and non‑culpable behaviour?

You answer: Culpable and non‑culpable behaviour? No, not really.

Your labour relations representative continues: Arriving late everyday could result in disciplinary action, but this situation is more complex than it seems. The poor quality of work could be due to non‑culpable reasons.

At the performance meeting, you and Julie draw up an action plan together to get her back on track. You meet with her on a regular basis to “check in.”

You and Julie agree she should take a course to brush up on her computer skills.

You consult with your labour relations representative, who agrees that being late for work is a culpable behaviour.

Your labour relations representative says: The rest is more complex. Is she willfully missing deadlines and producing reports that are of sub‑standard quality?

You answer: I do not think she does it on purpose. Now she says that she is under stress.

Your labour relations representative adds: How about if she signed a letter of basic expectations (working hours, lunch, breaks) as a way of addressing arriving late to work?

Your labour relations representative continues: On the TBS Web site, you consult the Employee Assistance Program, accommodation, and fitness-to-work evaluations.

Julie ends up taking stress leave for four weeks. You create a back-to-work plan with her and discuss how and when she would like to communicate with you while she is on leave. She ends up returning to work after six weeks and asks for reduced hours (with a later start time).

You tell Julie: We are going to use a team approach for working on documents.

Julie responds: Thanks, this approach lets me play to my strengths and allows me to seek help from team members where I still have improvements to make.
